Reading Passage 1
The Cognitive Benefits of Bilingualism

For decades, researchers have debated whether speaking two languages confers cognitive advantages beyond simple communication. Early studies in the 1960s suggested that bilingual children outperformed their monolingual peers on tests of mental flexibility and problem-solving. However, these findings were contested for much of the twentieth century, with critics arguing that the observed differences were artifacts of socioeconomic factors rather than true linguistic effects.

Recent neuroimaging studies have reinvigorated this debate. Researchers at the University of Edinburgh found that bilingual adults showed significantly denser gray matter in the anterior cingulate cortex, a region associated with executive function and attention control. This structural difference was more pronounced in individuals who had acquired both languages before the age of five, suggesting a critical developmental window during which bilingual experience reshapes the brain's architecture.

The proposed mechanism involves the constant management of two language systems. Unlike monolinguals, bilinguals must continuously inhibit one language while using the other — a mental workout that, over time, may strengthen the neural networks responsible for selective attention and cognitive flexibility. This hypothesis, sometimes called the bilingual advantage, predicts that bilinguals should excel at tasks requiring them to switch rapidly between competing stimuli or ignore irrelevant information.

Critics of the bilingual advantage hypothesis point to several failed replications. A 2014 meta-analysis of 63 studies found no consistent advantage for bilinguals on standardized executive function measures. The authors argued that publication bias — the tendency to publish positive results over null findings — had inflated early estimates of the effect. Moreover, bilingualism is not a monolithic phenomenon; the advantages, if they exist, may depend on factors such as the similarity of the two languages, frequency of code-switching, and socioeconomic background.

Despite these controversies, the practical implications of bilingualism research extend to aging and dementia. Several longitudinal studies have reported that the onset of Alzheimer's disease symptoms is delayed by an average of four to five years in bilingual patients compared to monolinguals with equivalent degrees of neuropathology. Researchers hypothesize that the lifelong cognitive exercise of managing two languages builds cognitive reserve — a buffer against the neurological damage caused by dementia.

QUESTION 3 OF 10
According to the passage, what can be inferred about the relationship between the age of bilingual language acquisition and structural brain differences?
A
Adults who learn a second language later in life experience no changes in brain structure whatsoever.
B
Learning two languages before age five is associated with more pronounced differences in brain density in regions linked to executive function.
C
The anterior cingulate cortex is only present in individuals who spoke two languages during childhood.
D
Structural brain differences between bilinguals and monolinguals disappear entirely after the age of five.
Correct Answer: B

The passage states that the structural difference in gray matter density "was more pronounced in individuals who had acquired both languages before the age of five." This directly supports option B. Option A is too absolute — the passage only says the differences are more pronounced with early acquisition, not that late learners show no changes. Options C and D are not supported by the text.
